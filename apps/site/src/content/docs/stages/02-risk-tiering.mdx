<GlossaryTerm term="Risk tier" /> classification is the gate that decides whether your AI system needs three pages of documentation or three hundred. Misclassify early and the rework cost compounds across every downstream stage.

<MermaidJourney currentStage={2} />

## The four tiers

The EU AI Act uses four tiers (unacceptable, high-risk, limited-risk, minimal-risk). Other frameworks differ — NIST AI RMF talks about "context" rather than discrete tiers; ISO/IEC 42001 leaves the tiering taxonomy to your AIMS. The discipline is the same: classify once, write down the reasoning, revisit when context changes.

<PersonaSection persona="exec">
The tier determines whether a system can ship in days or months. Push for clarity on this decision early — every week the classification stays ambiguous costs engineering velocity and counsel time.
</PersonaSection>

<PersonaSection persona="engineer">
Tiering is not based on model size or training-data volume. It is based on use-context: who the system affects, what decisions it influences, what the downside of error looks like. A 1B-parameter model deciding loan eligibility is high-risk; a 70B-parameter model summarising internal meeting notes is not.
</PersonaSection>

<PersonaSection persona="compliance">
EU AI Act Article 6 plus Annex III are the operative texts. Annex III lists eight high-risk use-case categories. Crucially, Article 6(3) lets you derogate from high-risk classification with a written and registered justification — that's a paper exercise you cannot afford to skip when you believe it applies.
</PersonaSection>

## Fictional company

[Aurora Insurance London](/stories/aurora-insurance/) tiers their claim-fraud detection model as high-risk under Annex III §5(b). Their LLM-based customer-service summariser is limited-risk (transparency obligations only). Both decisions are documented in a one-page tier rationale stored next to the model card.

<RegQuote source="eu-ai-act" article="Art-6" snapshot="2026-05-24" regulationName="EU AI Act">
An AI system shall be considered to be high-risk where both of the following conditions are fulfilled: (a) the AI system is intended to be used as a safety component of a product, or is itself a product, covered by the Union harmonisation legislation listed in Annex I; (b) the product whose safety component is the AI system, or the AI system itself as a product, is required to undergo a third-party conformity assessment.
</RegQuote>

**Why this matters:** misclassification is the single most expensive mistake in this entire journey — every downstream control depends on the tier. Get a second pair of eyes on Annex III mapping before you commit.
